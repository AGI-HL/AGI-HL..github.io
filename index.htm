AGI can be created now

 

Abstract

This paper proposes a new theory based on the mechanism of human intelligence to bridge the critical gap between the theory of artificial general intelligence (AGI) and the mechanism of human intelligence, achieving autonomy and the origin of concepts. Autonomy enables an individual or system to operate independently based on its own goals, etc., and the origin of concepts allows it to generate and evolve concepts to the human level on its own. The mechanism of human intelligence consists of five elements: perception, understanding, memory, value guidance, and initiative guidance, which work together. By imitating the mechanism of human intelligence, AGI can achieve autonomy and the origin of concepts, including adopting a new computer model to process raw information, using innate and acquired drives as internal motivations, and achieving autonomous recognition and use of commands. The origin of concepts is based on the collaboration of the five elements and forms and evolves concepts through autonomous feature extraction and other methods. This theory provides a supplement to the achieve of AGI at the level of human intelligence and also triggers thoughts on the universe and the mission of humanity.

 

Keywords: AGI, Consciousness, Autonomy, Concept Origin, Understanding, Value Guidance, Initiative Guidance, Human Intelligence, Humanoid Robot, Fuzzy Thinking.

 

Definitions of terms used in this paper:

1) Innate (everything): What is set before the system starts is called innate.

2) Acquired (everything): What is obtained after the system starts is called acquired.

3) Perception: Perception in this paper refers to the process by which an individual or system receives external information through the senses and generates the sensation of receiving that information, excluding recognition and understanding. The traditional theoretical definition of perception is collection and recognition, but recognition is an intelligent behavior involving consciousness. Mixing conscious and non-conscious behaviors is not suitable for system construction. Therefore, we separate recognition from perception and consider recognition as part of understanding.

It will generate behaviors, new memories, and new rules. Value guidance and initiative guidance participate in it to guide and constrain the results of understanding.

5) Initiative guidance is the autonomous initiation of behavior by humans, including various body signals, reminder signals of resource limitations, innate behaviors, and self-awareness.

This paper introduces the human intelligence mechanism into the AGI theory, establishes a similar intelligence mechanism in the AGI system, achieves autonomy and the origin of concepts, and solves the key defects of the current AGI theory.

2.2 Achieving Autonomy

Autonomy means that an individual or system can act, choose, and judge independently according to its own needs and internal mechanisms.

The value and initiative of the intelligent mechanism give birth to needs. Under this mechanism, the system can independently recognize commands, signals, and data through innate behaviors. Among them, self-awareness is the representative of the system. Self-awareness establishes an ownership relationship between commands and signals and itself by recognizing commands and signals. Later, through memory retrieval of this ownership relationship, self-awareness can independently use the command and signal again.

The achievement of autonomy in AGI involves four aspects: signal-triggered behavior, autonomous recognition and use of commands, imitation of self-awareness, and autonomous establishment of behaviors.

2.2.1 Behavior Triggering

Classification of behavior triggering:

1) Consciousness-triggered: behaviors planned by self-awareness;

2) External body information-triggered: information outside the body and various signals generated inside the body, including physiological needs, psychological interests, and other control instructions. External body information is the external trigger point for autonomous behavior. Such information can prompt individuals to generate various behaviors. For example, food can trigger the tendency to eat, a pungent smell can trigger the tendency to avoid, and seeing an animal can trigger the tendency to socialize.

3) Internal body need signal-triggered: basic need signals sent by the body, such as hunger and thirst, are one of the internal trigger points for autonomous behavior. Such signals prompt individuals to generate the motivation to pursue behaviors that satisfy their needs, manifested as the active exploration and approach to valuable goals. For example, the hunger signal stimulates the individual to search for food. In this process, the individual is not passively accepting external instructions but is autonomously driving behavior based on internal physiological needs, demonstrating the manifestation of autonomy in meeting basic survival needs. The behavior tendency triggered by need signals is not limited to the physiological level but also extends to the psychological level, such as the thirst for knowledge (curiosity) and the need for social interaction (sense of belonging). These psychological need signals also prompt individuals to actively participate in learning and social activities, further expanding the scope of autonomous behavior.

4) Self-awareness passive selection-triggered: When facing multiple behavior choices, self-awareness plays a key decision-making role, embodying the core feature of autonomy. In a multi-task parallel or resource-limited situation, self-awareness weighs and evaluates different behavior options based on the individual's values, goal priorities, and past experiences, and then makes the choice that best suits the overall interests of the individual. For example, when time is limited, an individual needs to choose between work tasks and leisure activities. Self-awareness will comprehensively consider factors such as the work deadline, task importance, personal fatigue level, and the attractiveness of leisure activities, and finally determine the behavior direction. This autonomous decision-making ability in conflict situations is an important manifestation of autonomy. It ensures that individual behavior has a clear purpose and adaptability and can maintain the coherence and effectiveness of behavior in a complex and changing environment.

 

2.2.2 Autonomous Recognition and Use

Recognition and use belong to autonomous understanding. Human understanding is to establish the existence of a newly encountered X based on the senses and establish a relationship between X and one's own memory and innate settings from the perspective of "similarities and differences" or the direct behavior result of X. This is recognition, that is, what it is. The behavior triggered by X is how humans respond to X, that is, how to respond. "What it is" + "How to respond" constitutes a complete understanding.

Formal expression of understanding:

x ∈ U, L(AGI, x) = {x, AGI update}, L = understanding = (what it is + how to respond + memory + value guidance + initiative guidance).

"What it is" is the existence of X in the current state established by the senses of AGI. AGI establishes a relationship between X and its own memory and innate settings from the perspective of "similarities and differences" or the direct behavior result of X.

"How to respond" is what behaviors AGI can perform on X in the current state. These behaviors may be innate or acquired.

When the AGI system is started for the first time, L = (innate behaviors + innate value). As AGI explores, L = (perception update + memory update + behavior update + value update + initiative update).

The mechanism of autonomous understanding is that the senses are the starting point and the basis for decision-making. Acquired information establishes a relationship with the innate through memory retrieval and behavior processing, and the self-awareness makes decisions on choices under the guidance and constraints of value and initiative. The technical points for information technology implementation are: the achievement of innate presets, the achievement of memory retrieval, and the information discrimination of self-awareness based on "similarities and differences".

We take commands as an example to illustrate the achievement of autonomous understanding. The first command of a human comes from innate behavior. Self-awareness masters the first command by perceiving and understanding this behavior, and so does AGI. For example:

1) The first learning of a command. The first occurrence of each command is necessarily contained in the innate behavior of the individual, such as the first cry of a baby. In this process, self-awareness not only perceives the behavior action (crying) emitted by itself but also receives feedback information from the surrounding environment (such as the soothing reaction of the parents) and the physiological and psychological changes (such as rapid breathing, emotional tension, etc.) in the body caused by the behavior. These multi-dimensional perceptual experiences together form a complete event situation and are recorded in memory. Writing memory will trigger the recognition of AGI, that is, the data written into memory by the sensor will be recognized and understood by various behaviors of AGI, and the results will be pushed to the self-awareness, and the self-awareness will establish a relationship between the data.

2) Self-awareness distinguishes data, signals, and commands according to the innate settings. For example, the state of data = 1, the state of signal = 2, and the state of command = 3. By different states, it can be recognized whether the current data is data, a command, or a signal. This difference in consciousness state becomes an important basis for self-awareness to recognize and understand commands. Each command and signal, through innate presets, will have different effects on itself. Through the different effects on itself, self-awareness can independently determine the types of commands and signals and record this association in memory. The next time the same signal or need appears, self-awareness will search through memory and find the associated "commands I know" and execute the command, thus achieving the autonomous use of commands. For example, the first cry is an innate behavior, and self-awareness establishes "I - cry command" in memory, so it can be autonomously used by self-awareness through memory retrieval the next time.

3) Behavior-reaction association and command and signal memory. Any memory retrieval of "behavior", "reaction", "command", and "signal" may retrieve the associated memory of these four elements and the relationship with "self". When other negative signals appear, if there is no innate associated behavior, the memory retrieval of self-awareness will retrieve the hunger memory, which is also a negative signal, and self-awareness will decide whether to use "crying" to resolve the new negative signal.

These three types of behaviors are all composed of data, commands, and signals in a certain combination way. Innate behavior is a preset combination way. The behavior learned later is a combination way of internal and external existing behaviors, which is a combination way after individual practice. The combined behavior is a conscious combination and change of the combination way of one's own existing behaviors or external behaviors by the individual.

Human learning is classified into: imitation, knowledge learning. Among them, imitation is a learning method for concrete behaviors, and one of the innate methods used in imitation is limb correspondence, for example, finger corresponding to finger, head corresponding to head. Knowledge learning is one of the abstract knowledge learning after learning language and writing, and the other is concrete behavior learning, which belongs to imitation.

The method for humans to consciously change behavior is: based on needs and the understanding of behavior, self-awareness selects and changes the combination content of the behavior. If there is a sudden, needless thought, it will be discarded by consciousness and will not enter the next stage.

The achievement method of AGI:

  1. Needs or signals have associated specific behavior content in memory.
  2. Each new behavior will be explored by the curious behavior, generating a curious exploration set associated with the behavior.
  3. Other behaviors may also process this behavior, generating an associated relationship set;
  4. Memory retrieval will retrieve the sets in 1), 2), and 3) and become part of the result set and part of the understanding.
  5. Self-awareness selects the memory retrieval results according to the rules and forms the final behavior to be executed by self-awareness.

2.3 Concept Origin

The origin of concepts means that an individual or system can generate concepts and evolve them to the human concept level according to its own mechanisms. The common collaboration of perception, understanding, memory, value guidance, and initiative guidance is the mechanism of concept origin. Among them, "self-awareness" needs to "remember" something before that thing can participate in thinking. "Remembering" is an innate behavior, which leads to the generation of concepts. "Remembering" is not long-term memory but can be actively called by consciousness in subsequent thinking. This "remembering" behavior that depends on the feature set leads to fuzzy thinking in humans. This section elaborates from the aspects of concept generation, the relationship between concepts and language, and the influence of multiple factors on concepts.

2.3.1 Concept Generation and Cognitive Construction

The root of human thinking is rich and diverse raw data, which mainly comes from the information of the external world and the information of the conscious world collected by individuals through various sensory organs, such as images, sounds, tactile sensations, smells, and tastes. These sensory inputs constitute the direct experience of individuals in the world and are the raw materials for the development of thinking activities. For example, when people stroll in the garden, the bright colors of the flowers (visual information), the gentle touch of the breeze on the face (tactile information), the clear singing of the birds (auditory information), and the fragrant aroma of the flowers (olfactory information) together form a multi-dimensional sensory information set, which becomes the basis for the brain to construct the cognition of the garden scene and the starting point for the formation and understanding of the concept of the garden in thinking activities. The raw data collected by the sensor is also the final judgment basis for the decision-making of self-awareness.

In the cognitive system of human intelligence, concept generation is a dynamic process based on perceptual experience. When an individual interacts with the outside world or the inner consciousness for the first time, after comparing the thing information with memory, the brain will extract the non-repetitive information according to a specific algorithm to form a minimum feature set, which is recognized as the original concept by self-awareness. This original concept is the "representation" of the thing in the subsequent recollection and thinking of consciousness and is the first building block of the cognitive edifice, laying the foundation for the construction of the entire knowledge system. For example, when seeing an apple for the first time, taking the main color and contour thumbnail as the minimum feature set, in the subsequent memory and thinking, this feature set is the representation of the apple, forming the original concept of "apple".

If there is a repetition in the feature set, self-awareness will screen out the non-repetitive part as a new feature set or reconstruct the original feature set according to new perceptual information.

When humans face commonalities, when they are valuable, appear frequently, or are numerous, in order to consciously remember the commonalities, the above method will also be used. This is the "classification" behavior, which leads to the generation of commonality concepts, and the difference characteristics of specific things will establish a relationship with this concept. For example, when an individual first contacts an apple and forms the original concept of the apple, including its round appearance, red skin, etc. But when later encountering different varieties of apples, such as green apples, self-awareness will take the common roundness as the concept of apple, and the non-repetitive features of red and green will also be incorporated into the concept system.

The achievement method of this mechanism by AGI:

1) Concept birth: The sensor writes the collected data into memory and simultaneously converts certain data into sensation signals according to the innate preset. Writing memory triggers memory retrieval and returns the retrieval results, which include the data set N that does not exist in the memory for thing X and the minimum feature set T generated from the set N. Self-awareness takes T as the representation of X in subsequent thinking. If there is no set N in the results, self-awareness generates the minimum feature set T from the data set of X according to specific principles. For example, T+ space-time coordinates form the representation of X, or the existing representation in memory + space-time coordinates form the representation of X.

2) Concept evolution: As the representation D of X, for the change of X itself, the representation does not need to change. When encountering the repetition of the feature set by other things, the representation needs to be changed to avoid the uniqueness of D from failing. When D is repeated with other representations, a repetition set Y is generated, and self-awareness re-extracts the representation D1 from the non-repetitive set. The remembering principle of self-awareness for Y, for example: value and occurrence frequency. Y becomes the representation of the commonality.

3) Memory retrieval method: AGI and humans face unlimited information. Although memory is also limited under limited resources, it far exceeds the storage capacity of traditional computers. The time-limited retrieval designed in this paper is the basis for the timely response of AGI. The method is as follows:

1> Retrieval is divided into two modes: active and passive. Active retrieval is initiated by AGI through a command, and this command is the "thinking" command of humans. Everyone can recall that the sensation of their own "thinking" command and "thinking" language in consciousness is different.

2> Passive retrieval is triggered when each sensor collects data and writes it into memory. Retrieval is carried out within the memory range related to the sensor. The retrieval results can be sorted by priority according to value and frequency of use. If the number of results is large, a signal will be sent to self-awareness indicating that there are still unshown results in the conscious space.

3> When AGI still pays attention to this point after the search results are returned, the search will continue from the pause point for time-limited search. Each time is the same time unit. This method can obtain results as quickly as possible under the constraints of the sensor and can be retrieved infinitely.

4> The active retrieval method is the same as the passive retrieval method. The difference is that active retrieval requires AGI to provide the search content and use the search command to execute.

2.3.2 The Relationship Evolution between Concepts and Language and Ontology

In the early stage of human cognitive development, that is, before the emergence of language, concepts were directly closely related to the sensory characteristics of things, and these characteristics became the core basis for individuals to recognize and distinguish things. For example, primitive humans or infants and young children form the original concepts of various fruits through the perception of the shape, color, smell, and other characteristics of different fruits. These concepts are purely based on sensory experience and do not depend on language symbols.

With the innate social behavior, because language is concise and efficient and can quickly convey the core content of concepts with a small amount of information, it gradually occupies the "representation" position through frequent use and the necessity of social interaction, and language and writing are thus born. For example, the word "sun" is not only a simple language symbol but also represents a series of sensory characteristics (such as roundness, brightness, warmth, etc.) of the celestial body that emits light and heat in the sky. A stable bridge is established between concepts and ontology through neural connections, which enables the perceived things to be quickly associated with the corresponding concepts and also enables the perception and understanding of the ontology of things through concepts.

2.3.3 The Influence Mechanism of Memory Retrieval on Concept Presentation

Memory retrieval plays a crucial guiding role in the application process of concepts. When an individual needs to call a concept, different retrieval strategies of the memory system will lead to completely different retrieval results. The brain memory determines the retrieval order according to the value attribute and frequency of use of the concept. Concepts with higher value (such as those closely related to basic needs such as survival, safety, and reproduction) and frequently used will be retrieved first. For example, in a dangerous situation, concepts related to "avoiding danger" will be quickly activated because these concepts have important value in the individual's survival experience and are often called.

This retrieval mechanism based on value and frequency ensures that individuals can quickly obtain the most relevant and useful concepts in the cognitive process, thereby improving thinking efficiency and the accuracy of decision-making. At the same time, this mechanism also reflects the adaptability of the human cognitive system. It can dynamically adjust the retrieval priority of concepts according to the individual's life experience and environmental needs, so that cognitive resources are reasonably allocated.

Concepts with a higher frequency of use also have an advantage in memory retrieval. Frequently used concepts are continuously strengthened in neural connections and can therefore be called first. For example, in daily life, concepts such as "food", "water", "family", and "friends" are often used. Due to their high frequency of use, relatively stable neural pathways are formed in the memory system, and they can be quickly retrieved when needed. This memory retrieval mechanism based on value and frequency ensures that individuals can quickly obtain the most relevant and useful concepts in the cognitive process, improving thinking efficiency and the accuracy of decision-making. It reflects the adaptability and efficiency of the human cognitive system. It can dynamically adjust the retrieval priority of concepts according to the individual's life experience and environmental needs, so that cognitive resources are reasonably allocated to better serve the individual's survival and development.

2.3.4 The Influence of Value Orientation on Concepts

Value is an internal signal system in the human intelligence mechanism, and its core function is to provide guidance for human survival and development. Essentially, value includes two levels: innate setting and acquired expansion. The innate value is closely related to human instinctive perception and is directly mapped to the individual's sensory experience through sensor data. For example, the body's perception of pain corresponds to a negative value signal, prompting the individual to avoid danger; while the sensation of comfort (such as warmth, fullness, etc.) represents a positive value, guiding the individual to pursue resources conducive to survival and reproduction.

Acquired value is gradually formed in the long-term interaction between the individual and the environment and acquires meaning through association with innate value. The social and cultural environment plays an important role in the shaping of acquired value. For example, in some cultures, respecting the elderly is regarded as a behavior with high value. This value concept is associated with the innate social needs (related to group survival and reproduction) or the reward and punishment system and becomes part of the individual's code of conduct.

2.3.5 The Innate Sampling Mechanism and the Principle of Cognitive Economy

When facing the complex external world, humans adopt an innate sampling mechanism to deal with the limited cognitive resources, which reflects the principle of cognitive economy. When facing a large number of things, such as a vast grassland, countless stars, or a vast expanse of sand, individuals will not explore each object one by one but will choose the easiest or most representative samples for study. For example, when observing a grassland, people usually first focus on the few nearest and most conspicuous grass plants. By observing these samples in detail and extracting their characteristics (such as the shape, color, and height of the grass), they then generalize these characteristics to the entire grassland to form an overall cognition of the grassland.

For a single object, such as a simple stone or a leaf, humans also use a partial sampling method. Since comprehensively exploring all the details of an object requires a lot of time and effort, and in most cases, by sampling part of it (such as the texture characteristics of the stone, the vein structure of the leaf), it is possible to quickly grasp the main characteristics of the object and then form a preliminary cognition of the object. This sampling strategy enables humans to obtain enough information to deal with environmental challenges under limited cognitive resources and also reflects the pursuit of efficiency in the evolution of the human cognitive system.

2.3.6 The Limitations of Memory and Thinking and Coping Strategies

The instantaneous memory capacity in human consciousness is extremely limited, which greatly affects the thinking process. In the case of non-deliberate memory, when an individual needs to recall an event or object, only a small number of key features can be extracted. These features, as memory cues, can help reconstruct the complete memory to a certain extent. However, due to the incompleteness of the information, thinking often appears fuzzy. For example, when recalling a travel experience, only a few landmark scenic spots or fragmentary scenes may be remembered, and other details become blurred.

To cope with the limitations of memory and thinking, humans have developed feature comparison strategies and fuzzy thinking strategies in the thinking process. When making conscious comparisons, individuals mainly rely on feature sets directly related to the senses or feature sets related to specific concepts. For example, when judging whether two fruits are similar, they will compare their appearance characteristics (such as shape, color, size), taste characteristics (such as sweetness, acidity), and other characteristics closely related to sensory experience; or based on the existing fruit concepts (such as "berry fruits", "stone fruits", etc.), compare their characteristic commonalities and differences under the corresponding concepts. This feature-based thinking strategy enables humans to perform effective comparison, classification, reasoning, and other thinking activities under limited memory resources.

2.3.7 The Thinking Processing Mechanism of Questions and Context Switching

For "question", which is an innate behavior of humans, its form is a specific signal, sensation, or state, and these forms are essentially the manifestations of innate signals. The most primitive "question" is the lack of data of thing X in a certain sense of a human being. For example, when encountering an apple for the first time, the brain only has visual data but no tactile, olfactory, and gustatory data, which will generate a "question" or "unknown" signal and then a curious signal, leading to curious exploration.

For "but" and other context switches, it is a concept that humans will "remember" only when the context changes. For example: the original plan was to hunt, but a mountain fire made hunting impossible, but delicious cooked food was discovered. This thinking processing mechanism reflects the adaptability and understanding ability of human thinking to complex context changes, enabling individuals to maintain coherent cognition and effective decision-making in a dynamically changing environment and better cope with various challenges and changes in life.
